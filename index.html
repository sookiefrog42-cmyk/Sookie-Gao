Wang Hong

Beijing, 1948 · Accountant · Factory worker · Storyteller

"We were so poor, yet so happy. Five or six children around a table, each hiding their handful of peanuts. That was our New Year."

Wang Hong was born in 1948 in Xuanwu District, Beijing — near Caishikou, the old execution ground of dynasties past. Her father worked for the People's Bank of China. Her mother had bound feet, loosened after Liberation into what she called "half-big feet." There were twelve children in the house. Noisy, hungry, and entirely alive.

The Great Leap Forward, 1959

The famine arrived. Boys received thirty jin of grain a month, girls less. "If that ran out, you drank soy sauce soup until your legs swelled — press your finger in and the dent stayed." Children went to the outskirts to pick willow buds and elm leaves to mix into the dough. A candy cost one fen. You could only receive sugar if you had hepatitis.

The Cultural Revolution

She was a Red Guard, but not the kind who destroyed things. When factions wanted to beat teachers she respected, she stood in front of them. She watched a classmate's father locked in a dark room until he confessed to something he hadn't done. Her husband received a big-character poster accusing him of secretly owning weapons from eight countries. "He laughed," she says. "He said: I don't even know anyone from eight countries."

Work and a life made

She graduated from a technical school in heating and gas engineering, was transferred to accounting because she was good at maths. She spent thirty years managing accounts for factories and industry guilds — retiring at 45, then continuing freelance until her mid-fifties. Across a table, she talked for hours — about peanuts at New Year, about feet that were bound and then unbound, about dancing the loyalty dance at Tiananmen. About a life that was never simple and was also, somehow, full.

Yang Ayi

Beijing · Age 85 · Retired · over fifty years married

"Women must not depend entirely on their husbands. Economic independence — that is the most important thing. I worked until I was over sixty."

Yang Ayi attended Beijing's First Girls' High School, where the principal — a general's wife named Zhang Naiyi, severely near-sighted — appeared in the corridors every day without teaching a single class. She entered university in 1961. She married a university classmate. In two years it will be their diamond wedding — sixty years together.

Her philosophy: "Never complain about each other. Face difficulties together. Never look down on the other person at any point."

Work and motherhood

Monthly wage: 44 yuan each. A nanny cost 13–14 yuan a month. She and her husband split the dawn scramble — waking the baby before sunrise, nursing her, carrying her onto a crowded bus, balancing her on the ticket counter until they reached the nursery. Once, walking home in heavy rain with the child on her back, she felt a particular exhaustion she still remembers: "I just hoped life would get a little easier. That's all."

Daily life at 85

She wakes early. Before rising she lies still and massages her entire body — a practice maintained nearly twenty years, drawn from Chinese medicine texts she reads for pleasure. After breakfast: calligraphy. She copies the Thousand Character Classic — one thousand non-repeating characters. She tends her plants. She watches every table tennis match she can find. In bed by ten.

Li Ayi

Xinjiang origin · UIBE graduate · Former telecom executive

"When I came back to the industry, I didn't recognise it. I had left when it was 2G. I came back when it was 4G."

Li Ayi grew up in Xinjiang and came to Beijing for university in 1996. She graduated from UIBE and worked in telecoms — managing interactive services for Channel V and Star TV (News Corporation's Asian satellite network), working at the edge of what "digital" meant before it meant everything.

The fall of Star TV

After internal ownership shifts, the China content budget was cut by seventy percent. Ratings fell. The channel was sold. Li Ayi was made redundant — newly a mother. Her mother-in-law sent a text saying she wouldn't return to help. The child's nursery closed at 5:30pm; the office was too far. Her husband's salary made it "logical" for him to keep working. She stopped.

What was lost

When she tried to return, the industry had rebuilt around technologies she hadn't used. Colleagues who stayed had become her seniors. "You become two different worlds," she says. "Gradually you're further and further apart."

Field Research · Beijing

Becoming Housewives — A Study of Highly Educated Women

Through in-depth interviews with four women in Beijing — all highly educated, all having navigated the intersection of career and family — this research traces the layered forces behind women's choices: institutional care gaps, invisible gender norms, and the quiet labour of negotiating one's own worth.

Research by Xishang Gao · Qualitative Interview Study

Abstract

Cultural expectations, social norms, family factors, economic considerations, and external pressures interact with each other, jointly influencing the career choices of highly educated women. This research reveals the complex causes behind this phenomenon and provides a theoretical basis for understanding the career development of highly educated women, promoting gender equality and harmonious social development.

I.

The care gap is institutional, not personal

Schools dismiss at 4pm; workplaces close at 6pm. Elderly parents are unwell. Husbands are on upward trajectories. The gap between these facts falls almost automatically on the woman. None of the women interviewed chose this — they ran out of other options.

II.

Gender norms operate invisibly

Women tended to blame their withdrawal on personal inadequacy. Men in the same households faced no equivalent pressure. The assumption that men's careers take priority was so normalised it barely appeared as an assumption at all.

III.

Educated women negotiate value differently

Some reframed their domestic position — staying connected, continuing to study, applying knowledge to parenting. Others struggled with invisibility. "I fix the boiler to save money," one said. "But it doesn't seem to be seen as something valuable."

IV.

The choice is never only a choice

Cultural expectations, absent childcare, and asymmetric household power interact to make one outcome far more likely than it appears. Recognising this is not about removing agency — it is about acknowledging the full weight of what these women carried.

"I worked because I wanted to."

"And sometimes, I rested."

Historical context & advocacy

被忽视的她们 — The Long History of Being Overlooked

The women in this archive did not simply fall silent by accident. Their voices were shaped — and suppressed — by centuries of systems that treated women's time, bodies, knowledge, and stories as secondary. Understanding this history is part of why listening matters.

75% of the world's unpaid care work is done by women

Cooking, childcare, elder care, cleaning — this labour is invisible to GDP and economic measurement, yet it sustains every society. Women who perform it are described as "not working."

Source: UN Women, Progress of the World's Women, 2015
19% of Wikipedia biographies are about women

The largest encyclopaedia in history reflects who society has chosen to remember. Women's knowledge, achievements, and lives are structurally underrepresented in the digital record — and in libraries long before that.

Source: Graells-Garrido et al., 2015; Wagner et al., 2015
4× more unpaid housework done by women than men in China

Even among dual-income couples, Chinese women perform four times the unpaid domestic labour of their male partners — a pattern consistent even among highly educated women.

Source: Chinese Women's Research Society Survey, 2018

A Timeline of Women in China

10th c.

Foot binding (缠足) begins — practised for approximately one thousand years across China. Girls as young as four had their toes broken and bound to achieve a "lotus foot" of 3–4 inches. An estimated one billion women were affected. The practice was not fully eradicated until the early 20th century. Ko, D. (2005). Cinderella's Sisters. University of California Press.

1919

May Fourth Movement — women's education and rights enter mainstream discourse for the first time. Intellectuals debate women's roles, arranged marriage, and foot binding. Many of the women in this archive were born in the decades just after this moment.

1950

Marriage Law of the PRC — legally abolished arranged marriages and gave women the right to divorce. Considered one of the most important legal reforms for women in Chinese history. Yet enforcement was uneven, especially in rural areas.

1966–76

Cultural Revolution — the state declared "Women can hold up half the sky" (妇女能顶半边天) and mobilised women into the workforce and political life. Yet domestic labour remained almost entirely women's responsibility, and women were among the first targets of denunciation campaigns. Rofel, L. (1999). Other Modernities. University of California Press.

1980

One-Child Policy introduced — enforced through quotas, fines, and in many documented cases, forced sterilisations and abortions. Women's bodies became sites of state governance. An estimated 336 million abortions were performed during the policy period. Greenhalgh, S. (2008). Just One Child. University of California Press.

1990s

Market reforms and mass layoffs (下岗) — when state-owned enterprises were restructured, women were disproportionately among the first laid off and were actively encouraged to "return to the home" (回家). This reversed decades of female workforce participation. Jin, Y. (2006). Rethinking the 'Iron Girls'. Gender & History, 18(3).

2007

"Leftover Women" (剩女) officially coined — China's Ministry of Education added the term to its official lexicon, applying it to educated, single women over 27. State media ran campaigns warning women that prioritising career over marriage made them "leftover." The label placed particular pressure on educated, urban women. Fincher, L.H. (2014). Leftover Women. Zed Books.

2011

Supreme Court Marriage Law interpretation — ruled that marital property belongs to whoever's name is on the deed. Since men's names were more commonly on property titles, this disproportionately stripped women of assets they had contributed to. Fincher, L.H. (2014). Leftover Women. Zed Books.

2016

PRC Anti-Domestic Violence Law enacted — China's first national legislation specifically addressing domestic violence. Prior to this, domestic violence was largely treated as a private family matter. The All-China Women's Federation had documented that one in four women experienced domestic violence in their marriage.

Patterns that persist

↑ 43% Elder women are more likely to live in poverty than elder men

Career interruptions for caregiving result in lower pensions and savings in old age. Women who spent years as full-time homemakers often have no independent income after divorce or widowhood.

Source: OECD (2017), Preventing Ageing Unequally; Global AgeWatch Index
1993 The year women were first required to be included in US drug trials

Before legal reform, most pharmaceutical and cardiovascular research was conducted on male subjects. Women's heart attack symptoms — which differ significantly from men's — went unrecognised for decades. Women's pain is still statistically undertreated in clinical settings.

Source: NIH Revitalization Act 1993; Holdcroft, A. (2007). Gender bias in research. BMJ, 334(7597).
20% The global gender pay gap — women earn 80 cents for every dollar earned by men

The gap widens for women who have taken career breaks for caregiving, and again in retirement. Among elder women, this compounded gap translates directly into poverty and dependency.

Source: ILO, Global Wage Report, 2018–19

Why this matters

The world loses something when we stop listening.

Elder women hold memories, crafts, languages, and ways of seeing that exist nowhere else. When we rush past them — too busy, too distracted — we lose irreplaceable pieces of human experience.

  • 📖
    Living history. They remember what no textbook captures — textures, smells, the exact words spoken.
  • 🌿
    Dignity through witnessing. Being truly heard is one of the deepest forms of respect we can offer.
  • 🌸
    Patterns across lives. When stories are placed beside each other, the social structures become visible — who bore the cost, and why.
  • ⏳
    Before it's too late. Every passing week, voices are lost forever. We archive now, for generations who cannot ask.

《扔扔好》

Written by Xishang Gao · A granddaughter's letter to a grandmother she barely knew

Preface

我和奶奶并称不上熟，她的屋子几乎没有我的照片，而是挂满了我姑姑家孩子的照片，因为姑姑家孩子是跟着她长大的。我们平时很少交流，只有过年这种大日子我们才会见面，就算见了面我们也和陌生人一样没什么可以聊的。

小时候，奶奶她一直在姑姑那边住，那时我还会给她写信因为我没有手机，我还记得当时把"奶奶"写成了"扔扔"，可我不会去她家，因为我就算去摆放她也像外人一样，看着她和姑姑一家其乐融融让我心里被说不上的滋味填满。每次去奶奶家脑子里只有赶快回家躺着的念头，但难道家不就是有家人的位置吗，那为什么我想赶快离开奶奶家呢？

后来姑姑去了美国，爸爸就让她搬到了我们小区。她和照顾她的阿姨住在另一个楼，虽然就和我们楼距离100米左右，但我长大后从来没有一个人拜访过她。每次和父亲一起去拜访她的时候，她总是不停地塞给我各种吃的，但那只是为了打破我们之间僵硬陌生的气氛。

前几年，她经历了人生中重要的手术，一个腰骨手术，当时身为家人我一次都没看过她。

这次的家书作业让我恰好有机会和她好好谈谈，聊聊近几年的往事。我不追求我们的关系拉近也不希望我们的关系变好，不必刻意追求这段关系，自然而然形成才最好。

Chapter I  ·  Xuanwu District, 1948

我叫Wang Hong，1948年出生在北京宣武区西草厂29号。那里离着菜市口儿特别近，算特别繁华的地方。那里也是过去朝代杀人的地方儿，把那些改枪毙的罪犯都集中在那点儿去杀了。

小时候我和姐姐妹妹就在家附近玩跳皮筋耍拐。所谓"拐"实际就是把羊腿骨关节做的玩具。玩时先将拐往地上随便一撒，将包抛向天空，在包没落之前将拐摆成相同的一面，摆成后，再接住落下的包，一次摆不成，可摆两次、三次。

我们家属于军属，特别优待军属，因为我哥哥参军，去了抗美援朝战场。所以我从小受到不少照顾，不仅可以不收学费，可以省下2块5的学费，连医疗费也是免费的。

Chapter II  ·  A Household of Twelve

虽然我母亲生了五个孩子但其实我们家有十二个孩子。我们家一共有六个男孩，六个女孩，人数都和部队的一个班差不多了，因为我母亲是续弦，所以和家里一些人是同父异母，但也还好，家里特别热闹。

我的母亲还是在旗的人，在旗就是过去的皇亲的七大姑八大姨，我结了婚以后跟着我的老头，他也是在旗的，所以规矩特多，吃饭不让说话，女孩子坐必须并着腿得坐。

"过春节的时候一人给半斤瓜子给二两花生，那时候就分给我一份就赶快藏起来……这穷日子过得也挺高兴的，五六个孩子围着热闹。"

我父亲去世还比较早那没有经济来源后怎么办？我们家里就靠糊纸袋挣钱，过去的药都是搁在方盒口袋里头。当时最大的才十一，我姐姐才十岁，更小的有七岁，四岁，还有一岁的。

我母亲还是裹脚的，她小时候儿在天津的时候开始裹脚，解放以后，她就慢慢儿的把脚给放开了，成了半大脚儿。

Chapter III  ·  The Three Years of Famine, 1959

中国自己搞大跃进，左倾错误极其严重，虚报产量，上层领导被胜利冲昏头脑。1959年粮食刚够自给，1960年、1961年、1962年三年天灾，严重缺粮。

"一天一斤，早上吃二两，中午吃四两，晚上吃四两。不够你就喝酱油汤，最后喝的都腿都浮肿了，因为营养不够，用手在腿上一摁一个坑。"

实在不行就上郊外去弄柳树芽和榆树的新长的叶子，拿回来和到面里充当点儿粮食。那时候就也有卖高级糖的，一毛钱一块高级糖，用来充饱和补充营养。就算是糖你也需要得了肝炎才可以得到半斤白糖，或者半斤红糖的补助，要不就得不到糖。

Chapter IV  ·  Military Training, Yanqing, 1970

当时强迫所有人都备战备荒，有备战的思想，所以所有人都要拉练。我们1970年6月份去的延庆，需要背着窝头去拉练，因为天气背着的窝头都长霉了，但是到了老乡家时我们说要给扔了，老乡们都说别扔，给他们吃，他们洗一洗后，再把那毛儿给揪揪就吃了。

我走的脚底下到晚上都得挑着泡，拿着一个针把泡一个个挑都磨起来。有时候走到半道儿，眼前一片黑就晕倒了，还得让别的同志来背我。

Chapter V  ·  School During the Cultural Revolution

文化大革命刚刚开始，我们学校同学们就有人打老师，指着老师，直接喊老师的名字。他们那帮人就打那种平常对同学要求严的老师，那些人就公报私仇。老师们还不能辞掉工作，你要辞了职就更证明那帮人的话，你也永远找不着工作。

"我当时是红卫兵，有时候组织批斗会，还有维护的老师，站在老师的一边……别看我现在不怎么爱说话，那时候在学校还算勇敢。"

当时各家各户的家里也有矛盾。我们旁边的院的姐俩，家里是资本家，当时批她们爸妈，这个家的姐姐就维护她爸妈。后来那家姐姐也被揪出来，也被挂牌子，被说是资产阶级的狗崽子。那家的妹妹就大胆的揭发，说自己父母吸人血还剥削人民。这是我亲眼看见的。

Chapter VI  ·  Mao Badges and the Loyalty Dance

那会儿每人都需要有勋章，毛主席的纪念章。每年谁要是带新颖的毛主席勋章，别人就觉得你这人特别好，进步特别好。家里也得挂着毛主席，一进门必须得有毛主席，上学工作的时候刚一开始上课的时候，老师就喊起立然后背毛主席语录，然后祝福毛主席：「祝毛主席，万寿无疆……」

当时还要会跳忠字舞，我还在天安门跳过，专门有基地练习跳舞的。你甭说那时候的生活还觉得充实，人们都怕自己落后，能知道要积极的往上。

"我从上中学的时候就喜欢养鱼……可是到文化大革命的时候就都抄家，看见谁家养鱼就给你把鱼都给你倒到地沟里头，因为资产阶级的生活方式才养鱼养鸟。"

Chapter VII  ·  Into the Factory

我毕业后直接进入工作岗位，因为我们学的是煤气热力，是由北京市公用事业局创办的中专学校。我刚一进厂的时候，我是青年小组长，每礼拜六我们不休息，也不给钱，白加班，从早上7点到晚上7点。电还有限制，为了省电白天停电，到晚上夜班才开灯，一直工作过了12点才给三毛钱的夜班费。一毛钱才够买一张电影票的。

当时我被分配到了北京阀门厂，上面让你先受工人阶级再教育，先下车间劳动，从基层开始做起。后来那个研究所正好缺一会计，我就仗着我在学校数学好，就被推荐调了过去。那时候我们都得服从领导分配，没有说你跳槽从单位跳到另一个单位，你要你自己跳也跳不成，就看领导怎么决定。一直到1979年，我才从车间出来，当时已经参加工作十年了。

Chapter VIII  ·  Pregnant at the Lathe

当时工作的时候老站在机床上，那工作台就像跟桌子这么高，一天得上下好几次，一天工作8小时一直保持这样的姿势，实打实的8个小时工作，不包括吃饭，吃饭就只有半个小时。

"最困难的时候，是怀着我儿子的时候，当时我儿子都七八个月了，我还得在床上干活，需要搬的东西分量至少得有25公斤。"

我当时是五好职工，需要每月都超额完成任务才能当，当上五好职工不给奖金，但会把我的相片挂在厂子门口。我因为不好好休息，太忙，导致血压特别高，一直遗传到现在还是血压高，从肚子里有我女儿的时候，血压也直没下来。那时候还不敢开假条除非迫不得已，因为给的病假条一开一个月，我为了上班表现自己积极都不请。

Chapter IX  ·  Regret and Hope

Academic Paper · Yuanpei College, Peking University

Becoming Housewives: A Social Process Analysis of Highly Educated Women

Xishang Gao

Abstract

This research focuses on the phenomenon of highly educated women choosing to become full-time housewives. By using in-depth interview methods to collect multi-case data and analysis through in-depth exploration, it is found that cultural expectations, social norms, family factors, economic considerations, psychological factors, and external pressures interact with each other, jointly influencing the career choices of highly educated women. The research aims to reveal the complex causes behind this phenomenon, provide a theoretical basis and practical reference for society to understand and support the career development of highly educated women, and promote gender equality and harmonious social development.

Keywords: Highly educated women; Full-time housewives; Career choices; Influencing factors; Gender

1. Introduction

In society, with the popularization of education and the significant improvement of women's educational attainment, highly educated women face many choices in balancing career development and family roles. The fact that some of them choose to become full-time housewives has attracted widespread attention. In-depth analysis of the influencing factors behind this phenomenon is of great significance for understanding the changes in gender roles in society and promoting the sustainable development of families and society.

Based on multiperspective such as sociology, gender studies, and psychology, this research uses qualitative research methods to explore the deep driving forces and constraints of highly educated women's career transformation, filling the gaps in existing research in terms of in-depth interviews and comprehensive analysis of multiple factors, and providing an empirical basis and theoretical support for subsequent research.

2. Literature Review

2.1 Cultural Expectations and Social Norms

In terms of cultural expectations and social norms, traditional gender concepts still have a latent influence in modern society. Public opinion and the cultural environment tend to assign more family care responsibilities to women, resulting in some highly educated women facing difficult choices between family and society during the critical period of career development (Gilbert, 1993).

2.2 Family

In terms of family, the needs of children's education and care, the health status of family members, etc. have become important variables. Especially when there is a lack of a family support system, women often have to put aside their career development to ensure the smooth operation of family functions (Hochschild & Machung, 1997).

2.3 Economic

In economic aspects, the cost of child-rearing, the family economic structure, and the differences in the recognition of the economic value of housework affect women's decisions. For example, a high family income may reduce women's economic motivation to work outside the home, while the neglect of the economic return of housework weakens women's willingness to persevere in their careers (Thaler & Sunstein, 2008).

2.4 Psychology

In psychological aspects, the pursuit of personal career achievement, self-identity, and psychological well-being prompts some women to seek value realization in the family field. Especially during the bottleneck period of career development, the transformation of family roles has become an alternative path to meet psychological needs (Simon, 1995).

2.5 Environment and Atmosphere

At the same time, the environment and atmosphere created by the extended family, social networks, and media communication indirectly influence women's career choices by strengthening or reshaping their concepts of career and family (Erickson, 2005). However, most existing studies focus on a single side, lacking in-depth interviews and integrated analysis, making it difficult to comprehensively present the complex picture of highly educated women's career transformation.

3. Research Methods and Design

This research uses in-depth interview method in qualitative research to explore the experiences and internal perceptions of highly educated women who have become full-time housewives. The research sample selects women who graduated from key universities or have higher degrees and have become full-time housewives.

The interview uses open-ended questions covering educational and career backgrounds, factors influencing the decision to become a full-time housewife, social and family influences, economic and psychological considerations, and the influence of the number of children — such as "Can you describe your educational and career experiences in detail?", "What factors prompted you to decide to become a full-time housewife?", "How do social norms and family expectations influence your choice?" The average interview duration is about 60–90 minutes. After the interview data is transcribed, the thematic analysis method is used for coding and analysis.

4. Thematic Analysis

4.1 The Lack of Care Support and the Institutional Background of Career–Family Conflict

The absence of care support, encompassing both human and financial aspects, is a primary factor driving highly educated women to become full-time housewives. This lack is exacerbated by the institutional arrangements that intensify the conflict between career and family. For example, the early school dismissal time (around 4pm) in the current education system conflicts with parents' working hours (usually ending at 5 or 6pm), creating a care gap that is difficult to fill. In contrast, in the first 30 years after the founding of New China, there was a system of socialised childcare. Cadre women sent their children to nurseries, and schools had more coordinated schedules with parents' work, reducing the care burden on families.

Li said: "I really wanted to keep working, but with no one to help take care of the baby and my job situation, I had no choice."

Take Li as an example. She graduated from the University of International Business and Economics and had a successful career in the communication industry. However, due to job losses caused by industry changes and the birth of her child, coupled with the lack of family support for childcare, she reluctantly became a full-time housewife. She made several attempts to return to work but ultimately had to give up due to the inability to balance work and family responsibilities.

4.2 The Influence of Gender Norms on Women's Career Choices

In the decision-making process of becoming full-time housewives, gender norms play a profound and often unnoticed role. Women tend to attribute their inability to balance family and career to personal factors, such as their own capabilities. However, this overlooks the fact that men rarely face the same choice of sacrificing their careers for family, and even sharing family care responsibilities is often not expected of them.

Zhang said: "My husband's job is always prioritised at home. Everyone thinks it's normal for him to be busy with work and not have to worry much about the housework."

This results in a cumulative advantage for men, allowing them to earn more and be less likely to be asked to give up their careers. This gender norm is deeply ingrained in society and has a significant impact on women's career choices.

4.3 How These Women Negotiate Their Value

The issue of value is a significant concern for highly educated women who become full-time housewives. Many fear that their value will be overlooked or underestimated.

Li said: "I do so much around the house, like fixing the boiler to save money, but it doesn't seem to be seen as something valuable."

On the other hand, Yang, with a bachelor's degree in English and postgraduate studies in related fields, approaches the issue of value from a more positive perspective. She emphasises that even when at home, one should not be isolated from society. She believes that highly educated women can bring greater value to family education.

Yang said: "I use my knowledge to help my kids grow in a better way. That's my value as a full-time mom."

5. Conclusions and Implications

This research reveals the interaction of multiple factors in the social process of highly educated women becoming full-time housewives. To promote gender equality and support the career development of highly educated women, society needs to take several measures. Firstly, it should strengthen the construction of family support systems, such as providing more childcare services and support for family caregivers. Secondly, it is necessary to re-evaluate the economic value of housework and improve women's economic status in the family. Thirdly, efforts should be made to change traditional gender concepts and create a more equal and inclusive social and cultural environment. Finally, providing more career development opportunities and training for women who wish to re-enter the workforce can help them better balance family and career.

Future research can expand the sample size and scope, conduct longitudinal studies, and explore more effective strategies to support the development of highly educated women.
